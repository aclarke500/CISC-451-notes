Topic 1: Data in Healthcare

Types of Data

  • Demographics
    • Includes age, sex, socio-economic status, insurance type, language, religion, living situation, family structure, location, and occupation.
  • Continuous Monitoring Data
    • Vital signs like heart rate, respiration rate, body temperature, and more.
  • Medications
    • Details on prescribed, over-the-counter drugs, illegal substances, and alcohol consumption.
    • Uses coding systems, such as DIN (Canada), NDC (USA), and RxNorm for standardized drug names.
  • Laboratory Results
    • Includes blood, urine, and other test results coded by LOINC.
  • Clinical Notes
    • Notes from various healthcare providers, including doctors, nurses, and specialists.
    • Encompasses discharge summaries, radiology reports, and more.
  • Medical Images
    • Includes X-rays, ultrasounds, CT scans, MRIs, and other imaging techniques.
  • Other Medical Measurements
    • Additional data like EEG and ECG readings.

Stakeholders in Healthcare

  • Patients
    • Primary recipients of healthcare, with needs focused on access to and quality of care.
  • Providers
    • Healthcare professionals and institutions delivering care.
    • Includes hospitals, clinics, and private practitioners.
  • Payers
    • Entities responsible for financing healthcare services, such as insurance companies and government programs.
  • Policymakers
    • Regulatory bodies and government entities setting healthcare policies.

Electronic Health Records (EHR)

  • Digital version of patients' paper charts.
  • Includes longitudinal records from healthcare providers across different encounters.
  • Advantages:
    • Improved accessibility and efficiency in managing patient data.
    • Supports continuity of care and facilitates healthcare analytics.
  • Challenges:
    • Pre-populated fields may contain outdated information.
    • Data inconsistencies and potential inaccuracies in clinical documentation.

Topic 2: Methodology in Mining Healthcare Data

Mining Structured Data

  • Data Types: Structured data includes organized information like demographics, lab results, and diagnoses.
  • Approach:
    • Feature extraction techniques to convert structured data into usable formats for analysis.
    • Applying machine learning models for tasks like phenotyping and risk prediction.

Mining Unstructured Data

  • Data Types: Unstructured data includes clinical notes, imaging reports, and other free-text formats.
  • Approach:
    • Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to interpret and analyze text data.
    • Image processing and computer vision for medical image analysis.

Data Modalities in EHR Systems

  • Combining Multiple Data Types:
    • Structured Data: Demographics, lab results, medication details.
    • Unstructured Data: Clinical notes, images, audio recordings.
  • Challenges:
    • Integrating heterogeneous data from various sources and formats.
    • Ensuring data compatibility across systems for effective analysis.

Topic 3: LLM in Healthcare

Applications of LLMs in Healthcare

  • Summarization
    • Summarizes doctor-patient dialogues for easy reference.
    • Generates concise visit summaries from patient notes.
  • Entity Recognition
    • Extracts Protected Health Information (PHI) from unstructured patient notes.
    • Identifies key medical terms and entities for data analysis.
  • Text Classification
    • Classifies clinical documents like PubMed abstracts by type and relevance.
    • Determines diagnoses or document categories based on text data.
  • Question Answering
    • Supports medical queries with specific answers based on clinical information.
    • Facilitates patient and professional inquiries on disease symptoms and treatment options.

Specialist Models for Healthcare

  • Customized LLM models for healthcare:
    • Summarization, Entity Recognition, Relation Extraction, Text Classification, and Question Answering models tailored for clinical data.
  • Examples of Biomedical LLMs:
    • BioGPT, PubMedBERT, ClinicalBERT, Med-PaLM, and SciBERT, which are pre-trained on medical and scientific literature for improved domain-specific understanding.

Challenges and Bias in LLM Applications

  • Bias and Fairness
    • LLMs can generate biased results based on demographic or training set limitations.
    • Important to evaluate LLM output to ensure it does not perpetuate healthcare disparities.
  • Data Privacy
    • LLMs handling patient data must comply with healthcare privacy standards like HIPAA.
    • Ensuring secure handling of Protected Health Information (PHI) is essential.
